U.S. to Resume Refugee Admissions; 11 Countries still Blocked

By Agence France - Presse posted October 25, 2017

After a 120-day ban, the United States will start accepting refugees, but arrivals from 11 high-risk countries, most of them home to Muslim majorities, will still be barred, officials announced on Tuesday, October 24.


The temporary ban, which since January President Donald Trump struggled to enforce and eventually prevailed after a Supreme Court decision in late June, required authorities to update security procedures and set stricter screening procedures.


As a result of the study, Jennifer Higgins, Associate Director of Refugees at the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services Agency, said applicants would face "enhanced" screening, including more detailed reviews of their social media presence and connections.


"The security of the American people is our highest priority," she told journalists in a briefing.


Late Tuesday, Trump issued a new executive order on refugees that replaced the expiring one that was part of his contentious travel ban which evoked a series of court challenges on the grounds that it threatened Muslims. The new order will go hand in hand with a sharp cut on Trump's refugee approvals.


For the fiscal year ending September 30, 2017, President Barack Obama set the refugee cap at 110,000.

But Trump cut that to 53,000 after becoming president, even as he struggled to put in place a full ban.

Trump has reduced the maximum number to 45,000 for fiscal year 2018.


Officials declined to list the 11 countries, but said they're the same as on a tougher screening list for 2015, requiring a "Security Advisory Opinion." They're now undergoing another 90-day security and intelligence review, but officials wouldn't say what might happen then.
